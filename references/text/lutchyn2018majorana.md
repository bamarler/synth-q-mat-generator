# REVIEWS

# Majorana zero modes in superconductor–semiconductor heterostructures

*R. M. Lutchyn1\*, E. P. A. M. Bakkers2,3, L. P. Kouwenhoven2,4, P. Krogstrup5, C. M. Marcus5 and Y. Oreg6*

Abstract | Realizing topological superconductivity and Majorana zero modes in the laboratory is a major goal in condensed-matter physics. In this Review, we survey the current status of this rapidly developing field, focusing on proposals for the realization of topological superconductivity in semiconductor–superconductor heterostructures. We examine materials science progress in growing InAs and InSb semiconductor nanowires and characterizing these systems. We then discuss the observation of robust signatures of Majorana zero modes in recent experiments, paying particular attention to zero-bias tunnelling conduction measurements and Coulomb blockade experiments. We also outline several next-generation experiments probing exotic properties of Majorana zero modes, including fusion rules and non-Abelian exchange statistics. Finally, we discuss prospects for implementing Majorana-based topological quantum computation.

The search for topological phases of matter has generated significant interest in physics, chemistry and materials science[1](#page-14-0)–[4](#page-14-1) . The realization of topological superconductors supporting Majorana zero-energy modes (also known as Majorana zero modes, MZMs) is of fundamental scientific importance and would have profound technological applications for quantum information processing. MZMs correspond to exotic neutral excitations of a superconductor comprising an equal superposition of an electron and a hole. In this sense, a solid-state Majorana quasiparticle, which is its own antiparticle, is closely related to the Majorana fermions that were first introduced by Ettore Majorana in the context of elementary particle physic[s5](#page-14-2) . A neutral excitation in a superconductor has a special property owing to the inherent particle–hole symmetry of the material: it is bound to zero energy, so that there is no cost to occupy such a state. Therefore, the presence of a large number of localized MZMs leads to an extensive ground-state degeneracy, which can be exploited for topological quantum computing purposes[6](#page-14-3),[7](#page-14-4) . Theor[y8,](#page-14-5)[9](#page-14-6) predicts that MZMs localized at defects obey exotic exchange statistics, similar to that of non-Abelian anyons, which are neither fermions nor bosons. This is by far the most interesting property of MZMs, because it means that exchanging the position of MZMs corresponds to a non-trivial transformation within the degenerate ground-state manifold and represents a non-commutative operation, which does not depend on the method or details of its execution. Therefore, such

an operation is topologically protected (that is, it only depends on the exchange statistics of the quasiparticles) and may be used to implement quantum gates. These ideas are at the heart of the topological approach to quantum computin[g6](#page-14-3)[,7](#page-14-4) . Indeed, by exploiting topological materials, which by their nature minimize error[s6](#page-14-3) , it might be possible to overcome the largest barrier to building scalable quantum computing: decoherence.

A number of platforms for realizing MZMs in the laboratory have been put forwar[d10–](#page-14-7)[18.](#page-14-8) Most of them propose to engineer an appropriate model Hamiltonian at the interface between a conventional superconductor and topological insulator[s19–](#page-14-9)[22](#page-14-10), semiconductors with strong spin–orbit couplin[g23–](#page-14-11)[29](#page-14-12) or magnetic atom chain[s30–](#page-14-13)[45.](#page-14-14) Among the most promising proposals are those based on proximitized nanowires[25](#page-14-15),[26;](#page-14-16) there is mounting experimental evidence for the presence of MZMs in these system[s46–](#page-14-17)[62](#page-14-18).

A paradigmatic model for a 1D topological superconductor involves spinless fermions that hop along a chain and experience proximity-induced *p*-wave superconducting pairin[g63.](#page-14-19) Such a system supports an odd number of localized MZMs at the opposite ends of the chain. As explained above, these modes are neutral and thus a single MZM cannot accommodate a conventional (Dirac) fermion. At least two MZMs are needed to form a fermionic state with a well-defined occupation number. As a result, a 1D topological superconductor supports a non-local fermionic mode comprising two MZMs

This non-local entanglement in a gapped system is the hallmark of topological superconductors and represents an instance of electron fractionalization63. This argument relies on having an odd number of MZMs per wire end, because an even number of Majorana modes can pair up and locally form an Andreev state (which is a conventional fermionic state). Such a fermionic mode generically resides at a finite energy, and as a result, there is no non-local entanglement in the ground state. Thus, there is a profound difference between conventional gapped superconductors, which have a unique ground state with even fermion parity, and topological superconductors, which instead have a highly degenerate ground state owing to the presence of many MZMs<sup>9,63</sup>.

Realizing the above model poses a materials science challenge. Spinless p-wave superconductivity is a key element for realizing separated MZMs. However, electrons in conventional materials have spin 1/2; thus, the notion of a spinless superconductor does not seem immediately relevant to real physical systems. An elegant way to overcome this difficulty is to use spin-orbit materials, in which the spin and orbital degrees of freedom are correlated. Indeed, spinless superconductivity can effectively emerge in a semiconductor nanowire with strong spinorbit interaction proximity-coupled to a conventional (s-wave) superconductor<sup>25,26</sup>. The energy spectrum for a nanowire with spin-orbit coupling is shown in FIG. 1a. The spin-orbit interaction shifts the momentum parabolas sideways, and when a magnetic field is applied, the Zeeman term (which does not commute with the spinorbit term) causes the opening of a gap in the spectrum at zero momentum (BOX 1). When the chemical potential  $\mu$  is tuned to be in the gap, the spectrum has a single band crossing Fermi points; thus, in this regime, the system is effectively described by 'spinless' degrees of freedom

(FIG. 1a). However, owing to the spin–orbit-induced rotation of the spins at the opposite Fermi points, the proximity-induced *s*-wave interaction opens a pairing gap in the spectrum. The resulting state is closely related to a spinless *p*-wave superconductor (BOX 1).

One of the virtues of this model is that the proximitized nanowire can be driven into a topological phase by tuning the magnetic field or the chemical potential (FIG. 1b). The emergence of MZMs at a certain critical value of a control parameter is necessarily accompanied by the closing of the bulk gap<sup>9,63</sup>, which corresponds to a topological quantum phase transition (that is, a quantum phase transition between topologically trivial and non-trivial states; FIG. 1b). The topological phase is stable with respect to small perturbations (such as disorder) as long as they do not cause the bulk gap in the spectrum to collapse. The ability to realize the topological phase depends on the effective spin-orbit energy  $E_{so}$ , the proximity-induced gap  $\Delta_0$  and the effective Zeeman energy  $V_z$  in the heterostructure. Therefore, when engineering materials to obtain MZMs, an obvious choice is to pick material components that individually have the required properties. So far, the heavy-element semiconductors InAs and InSb have received considerable attention owing to their strong spin-orbit coupling and large Landé g-factor, whereas Al and NbTiN have been used as the superconducting components. The relevant bulk properties of these materials are listed in TABLES 1, 2. The large g-factor of the semiconductor allows one to achieve the desired effect, opening a large effective Zeeman gap in the proximitized nanowire using in-plane magnetic fields with values below the critical field of the s-wave superconductor. High-quality interfaces between the two components lead to a significant hybridization of the semiconducting and metallic states, resulting in a large induced pairing gap. The interface should be smooth, because disorder scattering tends to suppress p-wave superconductivity and eventually leads to the collapse of the topological phase<sup>64–78</sup>. All these requirements are important for the observation of MZMs and pose a challenging materials science task

This Review is focused on the current state of the realization of MZMs in superconductor–semiconductor heterostructures; Majorana proposals based on topological insulators and chains of magnetic atoms have been covered in several other excellent reviews <sup>10,79–83</sup>. We start with an overview of the recent materials science developments in the growth of high-mobility semiconductors and in the preparation of high-quality interfaces between superconductors and semiconductors, which make it possible to perform experiments to detect MZMs. We then summarize robust experimental signatures of MZMs and discuss the best evidence for the presence of MZMs in proximitized nanowires. Finally, we discuss the perspective for topological quantum computation with MZMs and proposals for experiments in this area.

The quantum wells are grown close to the surface of the semiconductor, allowing for deposition or epitaxial growth of a superconducting material in close proximity to the well. The growth of 2D semiconductor materials has been studied for many years and is now well understood for many different material combinations. However, for semiconductor-superconductor heterostructures, new challenges emerge. To increase electron mobility in conventional 2D materials, an obvious strategy is to grow a thick insulating and, ideally, lattice-matched layer on top of the material hosting the 2D electron gas (2DEG) to protect the 2DEG from surface disorder. However, this also suppresses induced superconductivity, which relies on electron exchange between the semiconductor and superconductor. The solution is to grow a thinner and low-barrier material as an intermediate layer; the task is to find the barrier height and thickness that give the best combination of mobility and induced superconductivity84. Moreover, because 1D confinement is defined by top-down methods using either mesa etching or selective etching of the superconductor, the need for controlled definition of the nanowires leads to additional requirements on the materials and etching processes. This is likely to be a topic of ongoing research over the coming years.

The other way to produce nanowires is to use the bottom-up vapour-liquid-solid (VLS) growth mechanism. This mechanism has been developed over the past 50 years and can be used to fabricate nanowires from a variety of semiconductor materials<sup>85–90</sup>. InAs and InSb have shown promising behaviour in this fabrication scheme. Typically, a metal particle catalyst is used to

collect precursor material from the vapour phase and to establish local supersaturation. The catalyst becomes liquid when it forms a eutect with the precursor species, and when a critical supersaturation level is reached, solid material is precipitated at the liquid/substrate interface, resulting in layer-by-layer growth. This mechanism results in a crystalline nanowire with radius determined by the catalyst dimensions (FIG. 2a). Precursor material can directly impinge from the gas phase onto the catalyst surface, or it can first land on the substrate and subsequently diffuse towards the catalyst. While diffusing, the adatoms can be trapped, and when this happens on the side wall of the nanowire, it leads to radial growth. To fabricate 1D nanowires, it is important to suppress this effect. In the case of InSb nanowire growth, an additional problem is the low surface energy of Sb, which acts as a surfactant and therefore makes it difficult to initiate nanowire growth, because the wetting layer tends to form a 2D layer that blocks nanowire growth. One way to overcome this problem and to reduce radial growth is by growing a heterostructure. For example, an InP/InAs stem wire can be used to grow an InSb wire<sup>86–90</sup> (FIG. 2b-d). These wires can be a few micrometres long and have diameters of 80-100 nm. The crystal structures of nanowires grown by VLS are relatively easily investigated by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The defect-free cubic (zinc blende) structure of an InSb nanowire grown in the [111]B direction is shown in FIG. 2a-d. By contrast, defect-free InAs nanowires are typically grown in the hexagonal (wurtzite) crystal phase with a [0001]B axis. For next-generation Majorana experiments, more complex nanowire structures are required, for instance, trijunctions (T-junctions), as explained below. An important requirement is that the junction between the branches has a small reflection amplitude for an incoming electron. While such structures can be processed ex situ using top-down lithography in 2D materials, VLS-grown structures can be fabricated in situ using the crystallography of the substrate<sup>91–97</sup>. For example, instead of a (111) substrate, it is possible to use a (001) substrate in which trenches have been etched to expose the (111)B facets; this turns the growth direction away from the vertical. By using electron-beam lithography, the catalysts are defined on the sloped facets (FIG. 2e), and nanowires grow perpendicular to these facets in two equivalent < 111 > B directions (FIG. 2f). After growth of the InP stem, the InSb segment is grown. Because all wires are epitaxially connected to the substrate, they form a single crystal when they merge. For the merging process, there are two important aspects to be considered. First, the position of the catalysts should be chosen so that there is the appropriate offset (indicated as the junction offset in FIG. 2e). If the offset is too small, the catalyst particles merge together on one of the nanowire side facets, and a nanowire bridge forms. If the offset is too large, the wires miss each other. An optimum offset is equal to the wire radius. Second, the merging process itself is governed by radial growth, which, as mentioned above, takes place in competition with axial growth93. Radial growth results in an epitaxial shell and is responsible for the merging of two nearby wires to form crosses or hashtags (FIG. 2g,h). With this method, a predefined number of wires can be connected to form a network, and the dimensions and symmetry of the resulting structures can be tuned by changing the spacing of the trenches (FIG. 2e). The crossed wires have a single-crystal structure, as revealed by TEM studies 93,97,98.

**Superconducting interfaces.** First-generation Majorana experiments<sup>46,48-51</sup> showed a large subgap conductance, indicating the presence of unintended subgap states, which are detrimental to topological quantum computation because they degrade topological protection. A likely source of the so-called soft gap, or continuum of subgap states, is disorder at the

semiconductor–superconductor interface<sup>73</sup>. For devices based on Majorana quasiparticles, the native oxide that forms when the semiconducting wires are exposed to ambient air must be removed before deposition of the superconductive material. There has been significant progress in improving these interfaces, which are obtained by gently etching the oxide and optimizing the deposition of the superconductor<sup>53,59,99</sup>.

Another important step forward was taken by epitaxially growing thin Al films on pristine facets of the nanowires without breaking the vacuum<sup>99</sup>. This method of in situ superconductor growth yields further significant suppression of the subgap density of states 100. Subsequently, high-quality epitaxial superconductorsemiconductor interfaces have been realized in other heterostructures<sup>60,84,98,101,102</sup>. Typical values of the proximity-induced superconducting gap in different semiconductor-superconductor heterostructures are listed in TABLE 3. In situ shadow deposition, in which semiconductor nanowires are used as masks for deposition of the superconductor, can be used to define junctions in the superconductor without additional lithography and etching steps, avoiding detrimental disorder from top-down processing97,98.

The basic requirements for the Al film are that it should be thin enough to withstand high parallel magnetic fields without being driven to the normal state and that it should have a uniform morphology along the nanowire, which ensures translational invariance and avoids disorder-induced subgap states. The equilibrium shape of Al on a large, rigid InAs surface is not a continuous film but a dewetted droplet with a contact angle of about 60-80°. However, if the characteristic migration length of the Al adatoms is much shorter than the nominal thickness of the deposited Al, the Al can be kinetically locked to a continuous film. The key to obtaining a short adatom migration length is to lower the temperature sufficiently below room temperature. The specific temperature needed is highly dependent on the type of interface and type of structure. Moreover, because the native oxide stabilizes the film morphology, it is important to take the sample out of vacuum before it reaches the dewetting temperature, which for Al on InAs is typically below room temperature (the specific transition temperature depends on the type of interface). Unlike InAs growth, in which the crystal structure and morphology are locked once the wire is formed, the Al phase can easily recrystallize through grain growth transitions, which, at the right temperatures, move the grain boundaries while keeping the film continuous.

*Characterization of the heterostructures.* Together with strong spin–orbit coupling and induced superconductivity, a key requirement for MZMs is quasi-ballistic electron transport with a controlled odd number of occupied modes. In the ballistic regime, the motion of 1D-confined electrons is restricted to discrete energy bands, which results in quantized conductance plateaux. The observation of quantized conductance therefore provides direct evidence for quasi-ballistic transport in the nanowires and allows control of the mode occupation. However, this is difficult to achieve in nanowires because, in a quasi-1D geometry, impurities and scattering at the nanowire/contact interface result in an increased probability of reflection, which obscures the observation of quantized conductance. To create highly transparent contacts, InSb wires can be exposed to a S etch to remove the oxide layer before Cr/Au contact deposition. The resulting nanowire device consists of bottom gates on which a hexagonal boron nitride flake is deposited as a high-quality dielectric[103](#page-15-17) (Fig. [4a](#page-7-0), inset). The samples are mounted in a dilution refrigerator with a base temperature of 15mK. The differential conductance *G*

is measured as a function of gate voltage *V*gate and bias voltage *V*bias (Fig. [4a\)](#page-7-0). At zero bias voltage, an extended plateau is visible at *G*0=2*e*<sup>2</sup> /*h*, demonstrating ballistic transport through the lowest nanowire sub-band, with an extracted mean free path *l* ≈ 300nm. An additional small plateau at 2*G*0 is observed, indicating that the device has a small but finite energy splitting between the second and third sub-bands. At finite bias voltage, the two-terminal conductance is quantized in integer values of *G*0 only if both the source and drain chemical potentials lie within the same sub-band. This creates diamond-shaped regions of constant conductance, indicated by black dotted lines in Fig. [4a](#page-7-0). At the tip of the diamond, the two dotted lines cross when *V*bias is equal to the sub-band energy spacing ∆*E*sub, which is estimated to be ∆*E*sub ≈ 16meV.

Phase-coherent transport is a basic requirement for the interferometric readout schemes of the topological data, as described below. To study the transport properties of nanowire networks, the structures are transferred from the growth chip to the desired chip for device fabricatio[n104](#page-15-18) using a nanomanipulator in a scanning electron microscope (SEM). An SEM image of the contacted nanowire hashtag structure is shown in the inset of Fig. [4b](#page-7-0). In the magnetoconductance of the device (Fig. [4b\)](#page-7-0), Aharonov– Bohm oscillations can be clearly seen. As expected, the period of the oscillations scales with the area of the hashtag device, demonstrating phase-coherent transport through the device. From temperature-dependent measurements, a phase-coherence length of a few micrometres has been estimated[98.](#page-15-10)

As mentioned above, it is important to eliminate unintended subgap states in order to achieve a hard superconducting gap, which protects against thermal quasiparticle excitations. The density of states in the proximitized nanowire can be probed with tunnelling spectroscopy by measuring the differential conductance as a function of the source–drain voltage. A comparison between Al conventionally evaporated and epitaxially grown on InAs nanowires is shown in Fig. [4c](#page-7-0). With epitaxially grown Al, the ratio of the above-gap to subgap conductance is about 1:100. Another peculiar feature of MZMs is that they lead to the fractional Josephson effect. This effect corresponds to coherent tunnelling of single electrons between two topological superconductors forming a

Josephson junction. As a function of magnetic field, the spectrum of Andreev states is predicted to change from being  $2\pi$  periodic in units of superconductor flux quantum to  $4\pi$  periodic (FIG. 5c);  $2\pi$  periodicity corresponds to the tunnelling of Cooper pairs, whereas  $4\pi$ periodicity develops because of coherent single-electron tunnelling processes. The characteristic crossing of two Andreev states at zero energy is protected by fermion parity in the junction<sup>63</sup>. When parity is conserved, there is a true level crossing in the energy spectrum, leading to  $4\pi$  periodicity of the supercurrent. In practice, finite-size effects lead to a small avoided level crossing, and a dynamical change in the fermion parity may also occur owing to unpaired electrons in the system. Therefore, supercurrent measurements should be performed at sufficiently high frequency that these effects are avoided<sup>25,63,124-128</sup>

Another signature of MZMs appears when a semiconductor nanowire is proximitized by a floating superconductor (rather than a grounded superconductor as discussed so far), such that the overall system has a finite charging energy<sup>129–135</sup>. The corresponding Hamiltonian reads

$$H_{\rm C} = E_{\rm C} \left( \widehat{N} - \mathcal{N}_{\rm g} \right)^2 \tag{2}$$

where  $\widehat{N}$  is the total number of electrons in the mesoscopic system (island);  $E_{\rm C} = e^2/2C_{\Sigma}$  is the charging energy, where e and  $C_{\Sigma}$  are the electron charge and the total capacitance of the nanowire and its superconducting shell, respectively; and  $\mathcal{N}_{\rm g} = C_{\rm g}V_{\rm g}/e$  is the dimensionless gate voltage, where  $C_{\rm g}$  and  $V_{\rm g}$  are the gate capacitance and gate voltage, respectively.

In a conventional (s-wave) superconductor with an even number of particles, all electrons are paired in the ground state. Adding one more electron costs electrostatic energy as well as pairing energy  $\Delta(B)$ . Therefore, if  $\Delta(B) > E_C$ , the ground-state energy of the proximitized nanowire corresponds to an even-charge sector with 2e-periodic dependence on  $\mathcal{N}_{g}$  (FIG. 5d). The presence of a magnetic field leads to the suppression of the quasiparticle gap  $\Delta(B)$ , and the parity of the ground state can have even-charge and odd-charge sectors, but the ground state remains 2e periodic as long as  $B < B_c$ . In the topological phase  $(B > B_c)$ , the periodicity of the ground-state energy changes owing to the presence of MZMs and becomes 1e periodic129 (FIG. 5d). This periodicity, in combination with the presence of elastic transport through a Coulomb-blockaded (Majorana) island, provides evidence for MZMs in the proximitized nanowires133. By contrast, transport through a metallic island (that is, no MZMs) is dominated by inelastic processes136.

An interesting situation arises when a Coulomb-blockaded island contains several pairs of MZMs. When such an island is coupled to several normal leads, Majorana-induced ground-state degeneracy leads to an exotic multichannel Kondo effect<sup>137–139</sup>.

#### Zero-bias tunnelling conductance measurements.

Tunnelling spectroscopy is a conceptually simple method of measuring the local density of states and is well suited to detect reconstruction of the energy spectrum across a topological phase transition. Given that MZMs are localized at the ends of the nanowire,

the differential conductance measurement is expected to exhibit a robust zero-bias tunnelling conductance peak in the topological phase. A typical device layout for tunnelling spectroscopy measurement is shown in FIG. 6a. Low-bias transport in the normal metal-superconductor junction is predominantly determined by Andreev processes, in which an incident electron is reflected as a hole, and a Cooper pair is added to the superconducting condensate (FIG. 6b). In the non-topological phase ( $B < B_c$ ), these processes are suppressed, and the junction is weakly transparent. By contrast, the transmission probability approaches one in the topological phase  $^{110-115,140,141}$ . The presence of

MZMs leads to a resonant Andreev reflection, and as a result, the differential tunnelling conductance acquires a universal value of 2*e*<sup>2</sup> /*h* at temperatures that are low compared with tunnel broadenin[g110,](#page-15-23)[116,](#page-15-24)[117](#page-15-25).

Shortly after theoretical proposals for proximitized nanowires were publishe[d25,](#page-14-15)[26](#page-14-16), the emergence of a zero-bias peak in a finite magnetic field was observed[46](#page-14-17). Similar results were published in several subsequent studie[s48](#page-14-21)[–51](#page-14-22),[54](#page-14-31) on InSb and InAs nanowires. First-generation experiment[s46](#page-14-17),[48](#page-14-21)[–51](#page-14-22) reported the appearance of a zero-bias peak together with a closing of the bulk gap. The zero-bias feature persisted over a substantial range of magnetic fields and gate voltages and disappeared with suppression of the superconductivity. Furthermore, the zero-bias peak disappeared when the in-plane magnetic field was aligned transverse to the wire axis, indicating that the effect depended on the interplay of Zeeman and spin–orbit couplings. All these observations are consistent with the presence of MZMs. However, first-generation experiments also revealed a large subgap density of states due to interfacial disorder, which coexisted with the zero-bias feature. It is challenging to separate the two contributions, and various alternative interpretations have been proposed involving disorder-induced weak anti-localization[68,](#page-15-39)[125](#page-15-40),[142,](#page-15-41) the formation of quantum dots[143,](#page-15-42)[144](#page-15-43) or the presence of smooth confining potentials[145.](#page-15-44)

More recent experiments with improved deposited InSb/NbTiN interfaces[53](#page-14-23),[54,](#page-14-31) epitaxial InAs/Al nanowire[s55](#page-14-27) and 2D InAs/Al heterostructure[s56](#page-14-32),[57,](#page-14-26) and epitaxial InAs/Al and InSb/Al interfaces[58](#page-14-28)[,60](#page-14-25) showed a much lower subgap density of states (Fig. [6c\)](#page-9-0) as well as ballistic transport properties that make it possible to rule out the scenario of a disorder-induced zero-bias conductance peak. It is the materials science progress reviewed in this work that has enabled significant improvement in electric transport and allowed investigation of subtle transport properties, such as quantization of the zero-bias tunnelling conductance. For example, early experiments[46,](#page-14-17)[48–](#page-14-21)[51](#page-14-22) reported a zero-bias peak much smaller than the predicted value of 2*e*<sup>2</sup> /*h*, but recent improvements in sample quality have led to an increase in the intensity of the zero-bias conductance peak, and a *G* value on the order of *e*<sup>2</sup> /*h* was observed in InAs/Al and InSb/Al devices[58,](#page-14-28)[146](#page-15-45). Improved InSb/NbTiN samples have been used to map the topological phase diagram through the dependence of the zero-bias peak on the chemical potential and magnetic fiel[d53](#page-14-23),[54.](#page-14-31) These measurements contradict many false-positive hypotheses. However, Andreev bound states may exhibit similar signatures[141,](#page-15-38)[145.](#page-15-44) To distinguish between local Andreev bound states and delocalized MZMs, it is necessary to carry out a detailed analysis of how the observed signal depends on the temperature and tunnel couplin[g57,](#page-14-26)[128](#page-15-30) (which was recently reported experimentally in 1D and 2D heterostructure devices[57](#page-14-26)[,58\)](#page-14-28) or to consider more complex geometries, for instance, a quantum dot coupled to the end of a nanowir[e143](#page-15-42)[,144](#page-15-43)[,147](#page-15-46)[–152,](#page-16-5) which allows measurement of the non-locality of MZMs[61.](#page-14-33) Tunnelling conductance due to local Andreev states depends on the tunnel barrier height, whereas Majorana-induced zero-bias peak is robust with respect to the tunnel

coupling and in fact exhibits a conductance plateau close to 2*e*<sup>2</sup> /*h* (REF.[58](#page-14-28)) (Fig. [6d\)](#page-9-0).

Taken together, recent tunnelling spectroscopy measurements provide strong evidence for the presence of MZMs in semiconductor–superconductor heterostructures. The high quality of materials, as well as improved control in devices, opens the possibility of studying more exotic properties of MZMs.

*Coulomb blockade experiments with proximitized nanowires.* Improvements in the quality of interfaces in epitaxially grown proximitized nanowires open the possibility of studying the interplay of topological superconductivity and Coulomb blockade phenomen[a52,](#page-14-30)[153.](#page-16-6) The experimental device consists of a mesoscopic proximitized nanowire with a finite charging energy *E*C that is coupled to two normal leads (Fig. [7a\)](#page-11-0). In the Coulomb blockade regime, the charge on the island can be controlled by changing the gate voltage *V*g as well as by electrostatically tuning single-channel barriers. Both these changes enable investigation of the crossover from strong to weak Coulomb blockade regimes and study of quantum charge fluctuations in the island. Quantum charge fluctuations have an imprint of the physical state of the system and thus allow identification of the topological phas[e134](#page-15-47).

As explained above, physical observables change their dependence on the dimensionless gate voltage Ng across the topological phase transition[129,](#page-15-31) and the charge periodicity changes from 2*e* to 1*e*. Therefore, the two-terminal conductance through the island at zero bias is expected to exhibit a crossover between these two limits as a function of the magnetic field. Theor[y133](#page-15-33) predicts that the conductance is determined by different processes as the magnetic field is increased from zero, with a Cooper-pair-dominated regime for *B*<*B\**, a sequential tunnelling regime for *B\**<*B*<*B*<sup>c</sup> and a Majorana-dominated regime for *B*>*B*<sup>c</sup> (here, *B\** is the field at which the ground state can have different parity, that is, ∆(*B\**)=*E*C.) For *B*<*B\**, the ground state of the system involves only the even-parity sector. Therefore, the zero-bias conductance peaks appear at the degeneracy points between nearest even-charge states (Fig. [7b\)](#page-11-0). For *B*\* < *B* < *B*c, the ground state changes its parity between even-charge and odd-charge sectors as a function of Ng, and zero-bias conductance peaks mark the change in ground-state parity. Thus, each conductance peak splits into two at *B*=*B\** (Fig. [7b\)](#page-11-0), and this splitting increases with magnetic field until the field reaches *B*<sup>c</sup> . At these degeneracy points, the dominant conduction mechanism is the resonant transfer of single electrons rather than Cooper pairs. Finally, in the topological regime (*B*>*B*<sup>c</sup> ), conductance peaks occur at half-integer values of Ng, independent of the magnetic field (up to finite-size corrections). The non-local nature of topological ground-state degeneracy can be investigated by studying the change in the spacing of the Coulomb blockade peaks, which originates from an exponentially small Majorana wavefunction overlap. The corresponding finite splitting energy  $\delta E \propto \exp(-L/\xi)$  (where *L* is the wire length and  $\xi$ is the coherence length) leads to an energy difference for even-parity and odd-parity ground states and results in a small shift in the charge degeneracy points. #### Towards topological quantum computation

In the past 5 years, a new field of research into mesoscopic topological superconductivity has emerged 52,123,129-131,133-135,137-139,150,156-165, accompanied by an impressive growth in experimental explorations of various superconducting heterostructures 46,48-51,53,55,84,98-100,103,153,166. The interplay of mesoscopic and topological physics makes it possible to manipulate quantum information stored in topological (non-local) degrees of freedom in a way that minimizes decoherence effects. Remarkable experimental progress, rapid improvement in materials quality and an enhanced theoretical understanding of the microscopic details 167-170 are encouraging developments for the prospect of building topological qubits in superconductor–semiconductor heterostructures.

The first step is to establish that the exotic non-local properties of MZMs are indeed realized in the experimental system. A hallmark of topological states of matter is ground-state degeneracy, which has not been tested experimentally to date. Fusion and braiding rules associated, respectively, with bringing together two initially separated MZMs or exchanging their positions are characteristic of a topological phase and are of fundamental importance for topological quantum computing. Fusion and braiding experiments can be performed in a small network of nanowires (Fig. [8a,b\)](#page-12-0). Fusion experiments are simpler than braiding experiments because they do not require an exchange of MZM position but nevertheless reveal topological properties of the system. Fusion rules can be tested using a device consisting of two Majorana islands connected together and to the superconducting leads by gate-tuneable tunnel barrier[s156](#page-16-10)[,161](#page-16-16) (valves, Fig. [8a](#page-12-0)). The effective charging energy of each island can be reduced by lowering the barrier connecting it to the superconducting lead. We denote open (low barrier) and closed (high barrier) valves by '1' and '0', respectively. The string 010, for example, represents the configuration in which the left and right valves are closed and the middle valve is open. This configuration of valves effectively merges the left and right islands into a single island disconnected from the superconducting reservoirs. Two distinct sequences of open and closed valve configurations, differing only by one intermediate configuration, eventually lead to different final charge configurations on the island[s161.](#page-16-16) This difference descends from the fusion rules of non-Abelian MZMs. While the sequence *a*1: 010→000→101→111 leads to a definite deterministic charge configuration on the left and right islands, the sequence *a*2: 010→111→101→111 results in an equal superposition of two distinct charge distributions. A measurement of the charge on the island following an execution of sequence *a*1 should be deterministic. By contrast, after the execution of sequence *a*2, two charge configurations with equal probability are expected.

For braiding operations, three Majorana wires that form a T-junction (Fig. [8b\)](#page-12-0) are necessary[156](#page-16-10),[161.](#page-16-16) The elementary sequence requires controlling five valves, that is, keeping the left-most and right-most valves open and tuning the remaining three valves[161.](#page-16-16) Following the above notation, the sequence *b*1: 10001→11101→10111→10001 results in braiding of the two MZMs next to the operative left and right valves. Here, the numbering of valves goes from left to right, with the middle number representing the coupling of the middle island to the lead. The double braiding operation should always lead to a flip in the parity of the qubit, which is another non-trivial test.

At the heart of measurement-only topological quantum computation is a projective measurement of the joint fermion parity encoded in a pair of MZMs. In this scheme, an effective braiding between two non-Abelian anyons is achieved by measuring the topological charge of anyonic pairs (that is, fermion parity) rather than physically exchanging their position[173,](#page-16-20)[174.](#page-16-21) Interferometric measurement is a promising way to distinguish between even and odd fermion parities; see, for example, the device shown in Fig. [8c](#page-12-0). As explained above, transport through a proximitized nanowire in the Coulomb blockade regime changes its periodicity with the gate charge from 2*e* to 1*e* as a function of applied magnetic field. In the topological regime (large magnetic field), charge transport is dominated by coherent single-electron transmission through MZMs[129](#page-15-31)[,133](#page-15-33),[135,](#page-15-32) which is very different from the inelastic transport in a normal metal island, although both systems exhibit a 1*e* periodic signal. Thus, the interferometric measurement should be able to distinguish between different tunnelling processes: transmission through MZMs or tunnelling through some other delocalized subgap state. Transmission through MZMs preserves electron coherence and therefore should depend on the enclosed magnetic flux *Φ*, whereas other processes do not. Although this setup cannot access properties of topological qubits (because the MZM parities are fixed by the charging energy), such an experiment is a crucial test of the concept of measuring MZM states using coherent links and represents a basic building block of Majorana-based surfac[e159,](#page-16-22)[162,](#page-16-23)[163](#page-16-24) and colou[r175](#page-16-25) codes.

To perform quantum information processing, a mesoscopic superconducting island should support at least four MZMs that maintain ground-state degeneracy and have a fixed number of electrons owing to a large charging energy. The simplest topological qubit is depicted in Fig. [8d](#page-12-0). The projective measurement can be performed by coupling MZMs to a central semiconductor quantum dot and measuring, for example, the quantum capacitance of the system. Indeed, virtual exchange of electrons between the semiconductor quantum dot and Majorana island results in a parity-dependent contribution to the ground-state energy, which ultimately leads to a change in the quantum dot charge distributio[n164,](#page-16-19)[165](#page-16-11),[176](#page-16-26). In a more complex setup, Majorana islands supporting six MZMs open the possibility of performing a measurement-only braiding operation by the sequential measurement of the fermion parity of different pairs of MZM[s165.](#page-16-11)

Finally, an array of topological qubits coupled together through semiconducting quantum dots represents a scalable architecture for Majorana-based quantum computin[g165](#page-16-11) (Fig. [8e](#page-12-0)). Quantum computation is performed using the measurement-only protocol, which is facilitated by tuneable couplings between MZMs and nearby semiconductor quantum dots. A detailed protocol for implementation of Clifford gates using a limited set of projective measurements in this architecture has also been reported[165.](#page-16-11) Combining the topologically protected Clifford gates with the ability to produce and distil magic state[s177](#page-16-27)[–183](#page-16-28) should ultimately lead to universal quantum computation with MZMs.

#### Summary and outlook

The search for MZMs in superconductor–semiconductor heterostructures is evolving at a rapid pace and now encompasses a broad range of disciplines from materials science to condensed-matter physics and quantum information science. In less than a decade since the first theoretical proposals[25,](#page-14-15)[26,](#page-14-16) compelling evidence for MZMs in proximitized nanowires has accumulated. Most of the experiments searching for MZMs have focused on tunnelling spectroscopy. Recent work on InSb/Al proximitized nanowire[s58](#page-14-28) reported robust 2*e*<sup>2</sup> /*h* conductance, consistent with the presence of MZMs. The dependence of the zero-bias peak on the chemical potential and magnetic field has been measured, allowing the topological phase diagram to be mappe[d53](#page-14-23)[,54](#page-14-31). In a different approach, experiments on floating proximitized nanowire[s52](#page-14-30) set the stage for exploration of the Coulomb blockade effect in mesoscopic topological superconductors. The measurement of the 4π-periodic fractional Josephson effect was recently reported[62.](#page-14-18) Although these experiments strongly support the existence of emergent MZMs in proximitized nanowires, direct observation of the non-Abelian properties associated with Majorana quasiparticles is still lacking. We outlined future experiments for the detection of more exotic properties of MZMs, such as braiding and fusion rules, which rely on observing two MZMs at the ends of a single wire simultaneously.

Experiments with proximitized nanowires not only contribute to our understanding of topological phases of matter but also have significant theoretical and practical ramifications beyond condensed-matter physics. Next-generation experiments with Majorana nanowires will set the stage for the manipulation of quantum information in topological systems. Scalable designs for topological quantum computation based on superconducting islands hosting multiple pairs of MZMs have been put forward in the past 2 years[164,](#page-16-19)[165.](#page-16-11)

Experimental demonstration of the exotic physics associated with MZMs, such as non-Abelian statistics, would constitute a breakthrough for all of fundamental physics, as well as pave the way for the next milestone: validation of a topological qubit operating in the exponentially protected regime and exhibiting long coherenceal times[184](#page-16-29). These accomplishments would lead to the exploration of different ideas for topological quantum memories and fault-tolerant universal quantum computation.
